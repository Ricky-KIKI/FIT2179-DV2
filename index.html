Sometime tonight, somewhere in the world, a teenager will click play on a Japanese cartoon and forget where they are for twenty-three minutes. That moment is part of an export industry now larger than Japan's steel industry — one whose international revenue passed domestic revenue for the first time in 2020 and never went back.

But the people who drew those twenty-three minutes are clustered so densely in two Tokyo neighbourhoods that you could walk between most of the major studios in an afternoon. This is the story of an industry whose geography has not caught up with its global ambition.

01 An industry the world finally noticed

The numbers below come from the Association of Japanese Animations (AJA), which has tracked the industry annually since 2002. The pattern is the same every year: domestic Japan grows slowly, overseas grows fast, and the overseas share keeps climbing. In 2024 overseas alone hit ¥2.17 trillion ($14.25B).

02 Two wards along one train line

In 2016 the AJA conducted an industry-wide location survey — the most comprehensive ever attempted. It counted 622 companies involved in anime production. 542 of them — 87.1% — were headquartered in Tokyo. And inside Tokyo, two neighbouring wards (Suginami and Nerima) held 241 of those 542 between them.

Looked at another way: 38.7% of Japan's entire animation industry is packed into two wards that together cover less land area than the Brisbane CBD. The pattern is so dense that if you stand at Ogikubo station and walk for twenty minutes in any direction, you will pass studios that made works you know — Demon Slayer, Attack on Titan, Jujutsu Kaisen.

03 A family tree, not a list

The 622-studio number doesn't capture what's interesting about Japanese animation: it's a single extended family. Mushi Production trained Yoko Hatta, who founded Kyoto Animation. Madhouse co-founder Masao Maruyama left in 2011 to start MAPPA. Tatsunoko alumni founded Production I.G; ex-Production I.G producer Kenji Horikawa founded P.A. Works in Toyama. Almost every studio that matters today is two or three founders deep into Mushi.

05 An industry that earns and a workforce that doesn't

The story would be cleaner if it ended at "Japan built a $25 billion industry." It doesn't. The production studios — the ones doing the drawing — receive only 13% of that $25 billion. The rest goes to production committees: TV networks, publishers, and toy companies who fund anime in exchange for the IP rights.

Even as the industry hits records, studios fold. Eight closed between January and September 2025 — the third consecutive annual increase, on pace to match the all-time worst year of 2018.
